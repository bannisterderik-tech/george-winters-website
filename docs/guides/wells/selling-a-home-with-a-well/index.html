Selling a home with a well: what to prepare

The short answer

Sellers of well properties in Oregon are required to test domestic wells (bacteria, nitrate, arsenic) at sale — but smart sellers go further: assemble the well log, service records, and a recent flow test so the water reads as an asset instead of an open question. Ready files close smoother and defend price.

Assemble the water story

Before listing, gather: the well log, any flow tests, pump service and replacement records, treatment system documentation, and this transaction’s required quality testing. One folder, ready to hand over.

This does two jobs: it satisfies Oregon’s testing requirement cleanly, and it frames your property as maintained — buyers extrapolate from what they can see.

Fix the cheap stuff first

Wellhead presentation matters more than sellers expect: a proper cap, clear access, tidy grading, and a labeled pressure system photograph well and inspect better. If the pump is elderly, decide deliberately whether to replace, credit, or price accordingly — before a buyer decides for you.

Local rule of thumb: Hand buyers the water story before they ask, and you will keep control of the conversation.
FAQ

Related questions

When should the required testing happen?

Early enough that results are in hand for buyer review without delaying closing — we schedule it as part of listing preparation, not as a scramble.

What if my test comes back with an issue?

Address it head-on: many findings have established fixes (disinfection and repair for bacteria, treatment for others). A solved problem with documentation often lands better than an untested unknown.

Does a strong well add value?

A documented strong well absolutely supports value on rural property — it removes the buyer’s biggest unknown. Documentation is what converts the well from claim to asset.
