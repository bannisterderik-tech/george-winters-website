First-time buyers · Field guide

How much down payment do I really need?

The short answer

Twenty percent is a myth with good posture: plenty of established loan programs start far lower — including low- and no-down options for qualified buyers — and down payment assistance can stack on top. Twenty percent changes the math (no mortgage insurance, smaller payments) but it is an option, not a gate.

The actual landscape

Mainstream programs span a range of minimum down payments, from low-single-digit percentages on widely used loans to zero-down programs for qualifying situations. What changes with smaller down payments is arithmetic — mortgage insurance, payment size, equity pace — not eligibility for ownership itself.

This is precisely lender-conversation territory: your profile meets today’s programs in one appointment, and the real number for your situation replaces the folklore one.

Strategy over slogans

The wiser framing is total readiness: down payment plus closing costs plus reserves plus the move itself — sized against programs and assistance you actually qualify for. Waiting years to reach a mythical twenty sometimes costs more (in rent and missed equity) than buying sooner sensibly; sometimes waiting is right. Math decides, not maxims.

For valley-minded first-timers: rural properties layer their own budget lines (inspections, immediate systems work) — see the budgeting guide — but the down payment principles hold corridor-wide.

Local rule of thumb: Replace “someday when I have 20 percent” with one lender conversation about today.
FAQ

Related questions

Is mortgage insurance a ripoff?

It is a cost that buys earlier ownership — rationally weighed against rent and appreciation timelines, and removable down the road on many loans. Neutral math, not moral failing.

Can gifts count toward down payment?

Commonly yes within program documentation rules — standard practice your lender papers correctly. Family help launches many first purchases.

What about closing costs?

Budget them alongside the down payment — and know they are negotiable territory (seller credits) and assistance-program territory too. See the full budgeting guide.
