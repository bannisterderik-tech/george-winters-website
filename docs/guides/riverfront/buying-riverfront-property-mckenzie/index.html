Riverfront & flood · Field guide

How do I buy riverfront property on the McKenzie?

The short answer

Buying riverfront well means clearing six checks before you commit: the FEMA flood zone and its insurance cost, the bank and its setback rules, the water and septic systems, legal access and easements, the river’s erosion history on that reach, and early insurance quotes for both flood and fire. Romance after diligence — that is the order.

The six checks

Run them in roughly this order:

  • Floodplain first — confirm the FEMA zone and whether an elevation certificate exists; it drives insurance, lending, and future projects.
  • Know the bank — setback and riparian rules shape what you can build, cut, or add near the water. Verify with the county before you plan anything.
  • Water and septic lead the file — full rural diligence applies doubly near the water table.
  • Access and easements — who owns the drive, who maintains it, and is it recorded?
  • The river moves — ask about erosion history and bank work, and check what permits stand behind any armoring.
  • Insure it early — flood and fire quotes before you write, not at closing.

Reading frontage like a local

Not all frontage is equal: a high stable bank with filtered river views lives differently than low meadow frontage with gravel-bar access — and they carry different risk, insurance, and joy profiles. Current speed, channel position, and sun across the water all shape daily life more than the listing photos admit.

This is precisely where walking the property with someone who knows the river pays for itself. I read banks for a living; I will tell you what a specific frontage is and is not before you fall in love.

Local rule of thumb: Buy the bank, not just the view — the bank is what you will maintain, insure, and live with.
FAQ

Related questions

Is riverfront worth the premium?

For the right owner, emphatically — frontage is the scarcest asset in the valley and the heart of its lifestyle. The premium deserves the full six-check diligence so you are paying for reality, not just imagery.

Can I put a dock or steps down the bank?

Work near or in the water typically involves permitting layers — county, state, sometimes federal. Never assume; we verify what is possible for the specific bank before that dream drives your offer.

Do all riverfront homes need flood insurance?

No — plenty sit above mapped flood zones. Requirements depend on the zone and your lender; costs depend on elevation details. That is why zone confirmation and early quotes lead the checklist.
