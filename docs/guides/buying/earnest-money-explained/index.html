Buying · Field guide

Earnest money, explained without the mystery

The short answer

Earnest money is your deposit demonstrating serious intent — held in escrow (not by the seller), credited to you at closing, and protected during the transaction by your contingencies. Its size signals commitment; its safety depends on the contract. Both deserve deliberate choices rather than defaults.

Mechanics and protection

On acceptance, your deposit goes to a neutral escrow holder and waits. Close normally and it becomes part of your funds at settlement. Terminate properly under a contingency — inspection, financing, appraisal, title — and it returns. The danger zone is walking away outside your contractual rights, where the deposit is the classic stake.

This is why contingency structure and deadlines are not paperwork trivia: they are the fence around your money. I watch those dates like they are my own deposit, because professionally they are.

Sizing as strategy

Right-sized earnest money is market- and situation-dependent: enough to signal seriousness for the property and competition, structured with your protections intact. In multiple-offer moments, deposit strength (and occasional staged increases) speaks; in quiet negotiations, standard sums serve.

Never confuse the deposit with the down payment — earnest money is simply the early-arriving part of funds you were bringing anyway, doing signaling work en route.

Local rule of thumb: Size the deposit for the message; guard it with the deadlines.
FAQ

Related questions

Who holds earnest money in Oregon?

Neutral escrow — typically title/escrow companies — under the transaction’s instructions. Sellers do not hold it, which protects everyone.

When can a seller keep it?

Broadly: when a buyer fails to perform without a contractual right to exit. Honoring contingency deadlines is the practical protection; calendared diligence is how we honor them.

Is more earnest money ever unwise?

Oversized deposits without corresponding protections add risk without proportional gain. Strategy is matching signal to situation — not maximalism.
